# Write a Client Final Deck like a Tier-1 Strategy Engagement Manager

> One Governing Thought, ten action titles, zero topic words.

![consulting-final-deck methodology illustration](https://cdn1.genspark.ai/user-upload-image/slide_agent/v2-catalog-hero/083-mckinsey-final-deliverable.png)

## Why this skill works

- **The CEO reads slide 02 and 18 and has the meeting.** Pyramid Principle means the Governing Thought is on slide 02; the roadmap is on slide 18; everything in between is supporting evidence the client can audit later.
- **By next quarter the workplans exist with owners.** Action-titled body slides mean each headline is already a decision; the implementation slide names who, what, and by when for every workstream.
- **You bring the engagement scope + the data you've gathered.** Tell the agent the client question, the analyses run, and the recommendations; it returns a deck that reads on the screen like a Partner wrote it and reads in PDF like a Director reviewed it.

## Methodology cheat-sheet

**Pyramid Principle × SCQA × Action Titles** — the three habits that distinguish a Partner-grade deck from a competent associate's deck.

1. **Pyramid Principle**[^1] — Barbara Minto's framework, developed in the 1970s and codified in *The Pyramid Principle* (1987). Every document answers a question; the answer is a single Governing Thought at the top; beneath are 3-4 supporting arguments that are Mutually Exclusive and Collectively Exhaustive (MECE); beneath those are evidence-level points. The deck's structure on slides 02-17 follows this directly: slide 02 is the top of the pyramid; chapters 04-08, 09-13, 14-17 are the three Top-of-Pyramid limbs.

2. **SCQA opening**[^1][^2] — Situation (what the reader already accepts), Complication (what changed), Question (what they're now asking), Answer (the Governing Thought). The opening sequence on slides 02-03 follows SCQA: slide 02 leads with the Answer (per Minto, the Answer comes first); slide 03 lays out S-C-Q for those who need orientation. SCQA also drives any meta-introduction the Partner gives verbally.

3. **Action titles**[^3] — the convention that every slide title is a full sentence stating the slide's conclusion. Pioneered jointly across the MBB firms; the operational test is "if the client read only the action titles in sequence, would they have the entire argument?" If yes, the deck is well-structured. If no, the action titles are rewritten until they pass.

4. **Zelazny chart discipline**[^4] — Gene Zelazny's *Say It With Charts* (multiple editions) is the field-standard guide for matching chart type to comparison type. The chart-choice checklist on slide 10 enforces this.

5. **The single deck-killing failure mode** — chronological narrative ("First we did this, then we did this…"). Minto's rule: the deck is structured by the *logic* of the argument, not by the *sequence* of the engagement. The Approach slide (slide 03) is the only slide where chronology is acceptable; everywhere else, the deck moves from argument to evidence.

## Before / After

### Opening a CXO final readout on a North America entry strategy

**Typical PPT template**

> **North America Strategic Options Review**
>
> - Executive Summary
> - Project Background
> - Methodology
> - Findings
> - Recommendations

**This skill's rewrite (slide 02, Governing Thought + Top-of-Pyramid)**

> **Enter North America via acquisition of Distributor B by Q3 2026 — $480M EBITDA addition by Year 3 at 14% IRR.**
>
> 1. The North American opportunity is **$24B addressable, 11% growth, concentrated in 3 segments** where our European product fit is highest.
>
> 2. Build-vs-buy strongly favors acquisition: organic build is **5 years to break-even** versus **18 months via Distributor B**, which already owns 60% of two priority segments.
>
> 3. Execution is non-trivial but bounded: three risks (regulatory, talent retention, integration cost) total **$95M downside in the P50 scenario**, with mitigations on slides 16-17.

### Closing slide of the same readout

**Typical PPT template**

> **Conclusion**
>
> We believe North America represents a significant strategic opportunity. We recommend proceeding with detailed planning and stakeholder alignment in the coming quarters.

**This skill's rewrite (slide 18, Implementation Roadmap)**

> **Day 1 — Day 30 — Day 90 — Day 365:**
>
> | Workstream | Day 1 | Day 30 | Day 90 | Day 365 |
> |---|---|---|---|---|
> | Acquisition execution | LOI by 30 Jun (CFO) | SPA signed (CFO + GC) | Close + Day-1 readiness (COO) | Integrated P&L (CFO) |
> | Commercial integration | Sales-team mapping (CCO) | Joint pipeline review (CCO) | First quarter of joint orders (CCO) | $120M run-rate (CCO) |
> | Talent retention | Top-20 retention package (CHRO) | Performance-equity terms (CHRO) | 90-day pulse survey (CHRO) | < 8% top-talent attrition (CHRO) |
> | Regulatory | HSR filing (GC) | Second request response (GC) | Clearance (GC) | n/a |
>
> Escalation: weekly to CEO, monthly to Audit + Strategy Committee. Next checkpoint: 30-day post-close, board.

## What this skill produces

A 20-slide, 1920×1080 HTML deck for a 60-90 minute Partner-to-CXO final readout, doubling as a stand-alone PDF the client circulates internally. The deck enforces three non-negotiable disciplines drawn from Minto's Pyramid Principle[^1], SCQA[^1] [^2], and the action-title tradition codified across MBB tier-1 consulting[^3]:

1. **Governing Thought + Top-of-Pyramid on slide 02** — the Governing Thought is the single answer to the client's question, stated as a declarative sentence. Beneath it, three (rarely four) Top-of-Pyramid arguments that MECE-collectively prove the Governing Thought. The rest of the deck is the second and third layer beneath these.
2. **Action titles on every body slide** — every body slide title is a full sentence stating the slide's conclusion ("North American distribution adds $480M EBITDA at 14% IRR — but only if we acquire Distributor B by Q3"), never a topic ("North American distribution"). Topic titles are flagged red and rewritten.
3. **Implementation Roadmap on slide 18** — every workstream has an owner (client side, with name placeholder), a Day-1 / Day-30 / Day-90 milestone, a measurement, and an escalation path. The Roadmap converts the deck into a workplan within 7 days of the readout.

[^1]: Barbara Minto, *The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking.* Pearson Education, 3rd edition 2009 (original 1987). Foundational text for the deck's structure. https://www.barbaraminto.com/

[^2]: Barbara Minto, *The Minto Pyramid Principle* (summarised in the published book above). SCQA opening structure.

[^3]: Mehrdad Baghai, Stephen Coley, David White, *The Alchemy of Growth.* Perseus, 2000 — a published example by tier-1 strategy consultants of action-title and Pyramid-structured book-length argument; useful as a public exemplar. (For a public counterpart, see also Carl W. Stern & George Stalk, *Perspectives on Strategy from The Boston Consulting Group*, Wiley 1998.)

[^4]: Gene Zelazny, *Say It with Charts: The Executive's Guide to Visual Communication.* McGraw-Hill, 4th edition 2001 (and *Say It with Presentations*, 2006). The field-standard chart-choice canon. https://www.amazon.com/Say-Charts-Executives-Visual-Communication/dp/0071369988

[^5]: Edward R. Tufte, *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information.* Graphics Press, 2nd edition 2001. Reinforces the data-ink ratio principle that informs the deck's chart styling.

[^6]: Ethan Rasiel, *The McKinsey Way.* McGraw-Hill, 1999. Useful for the public articulation of tier-1 strategy communication discipline including action titles, MECE, and the 80/20 page habit.

[^7]: Chip Heath & Dan Heath, *Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die.* Random House, 2007. The SUCCESS framework reinforces why Governing Thoughts are memorable; useful for sharpening slide 02.
