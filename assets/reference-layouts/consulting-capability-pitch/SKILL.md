# Write a Consulting Capability Pitch like an MBB Partner Beating Big 4

> Answer 'why us, not Big 4' on slide 02 — not slide 20.

![consulting-capability-pitch methodology illustration](https://cdn1.genspark.ai/user-upload-image/slide_agent/v2-catalog-hero/086-mbb-capability-pitch.png)

## Why this skill works

- **The CFO leans forward at slide 02.** A Right-to-Win statement names the three specific reasons this firm wins this engagement against Big 4 — not generic excellence, not "we have a great team", but situation-specific differentiation.
- **By the end of the meeting they ask for a follow-up, not a price list.** Proof Stack of three named comparable cases with verifiable outcomes shifts the conversation from "can you do it" to "when do we start".
- **You bring the prospect's situation + your firm's three best comparable engagements.** Tell the agent the client's industry, the engagement type, and the three best analogues; it returns a pitch that survives a 90-minute panel against Big 4.

## Methodology cheat-sheet

**Right-to-Win × Proof Stack × Named Team** — three habits that distinguish a winning capability pitch from a generic firm-credentials deck.

1. **Right-to-Win discipline**[^1] — the discipline of stating, on the second slide, the three (rarely more) specific reasons this firm wins this engagement. Codified across MBB pursuit teams and increasingly across Big 4 strategy practices. The test: would a senior consultant at the competing firm read your slide 02 and say "we cannot match that"? If no, the slide is generic.

2. **Challenger Sale**[^3] — Dixon & Adamson, *The Challenger Sale* (2011). Top-performing complex-B2B sellers don't relationship-build their way in; they *teach, tailor, and take control* of the buying conversation. The deck operationalizes "teach" on slide 04 (Differentiation — what this firm sees that the prospect hasn't articulated), "tailor" on slide 02 (Right-to-Win), and "take control" on slide 16 (Decisions Required).

3. **Proof Stack discipline**[^2] — MBB internal training emphasizes that every capability claim must be tied to a specific, recent, named engagement with a quantified outcome. "We have done many such projects" is replaced with three named engagements with outcomes. The deck enforces one comparable per slide on 06-08 because a single rich case beats five thin cases.

4. **The named-team commitment**[^4] — procurement teams routinely catch the "phantom partner" trick (a senior partner named on the pitch but not on the engagement). The deck's slide 13 names individuals with the explicit commitment that they will work the engagement, and slide 14 has fee structures that activate only with the named individuals.

5. **The single deck-killing failure mode** — opening with firm credentials ("Founded in 1973, we operate in 60 countries…"). The CFO has read those slides before. The pitch is won when the pitch shows the CFO something about *their* business they didn't already know.

## Before / After

### Opening a beauty parade for a specialty-chemicals revenue-growth strategy

**Typical PPT template**

> **About [Firm Name]: Your Trusted Strategic Partner**
>
> - Founded 1973
> - 60+ offices globally
> - $X billion in revenue
> - Industry-leading methodologies
> - Award-winning client service

**This skill's rewrite (slide 02, Right-to-Win)**

> **Three reasons we win this engagement.**
>
> 1. **We have built a revenue-growth model on three specialty-chemicals businesses in the last 36 months** — including one that overlaps 60% of [Prospect]'s SKU portfolio. Outcomes on slides 06-08.
>
> 2. **The engagement is led by a partner who ran ChemCorp's APAC pricing transformation** (2023, +$180M EBITDA, 22% IRR) — same business model, same channel structure as [Prospect]'s. Bio on slide 13.
>
> 3. **A 6-person team — partner-led, 14 weeks, fixed fee.** Compare to a 14-person Big-4 team where partner attention drops after week 4; our partner is in the room every Tuesday. Commercials on slide 14.

### Closing slide of the same pitch

**Typical PPT template**

> **Thank You**
>
> We look forward to the opportunity to work with you. Please reach out with any questions about our proposal. We are committed to your success.

**This skill's rewrite (slide 16, Decisions Required)**

> **What we are asking the SteerCo to decide:**
>
> 1. **Confirm we are advancing to a Day-1 readiness session before 30 June.** This locks the start date and our 14-week path closes by 31 Dec.
>
> 2. **Approve a 2-hour pre-kickoff with the named partner and your CRO** in the next 10 business days, at our cost. We use this to refine the workplan and identify Day-1 quick wins (typical: 2-3 quick wins worth >$10M in-quarter).
>
> 3. **Confirm reference-call timing.** We have offered three reference contacts on slides 06-08; we ask you to take at least one this week so the decision can close on Friday.

## What this skill produces

A 16-slide, 1920×1080 HTML deck for a 60-90 minute competitive proposal pitch or beauty parade, doubling as the leave-behind the procurement team scores. The deck enforces three non-negotiable disciplines drawn from MBB proposal-development tradition[^1][^2], the Challenger Sale[^3], and the consulting-procurement scoring rubric tradition[^4]:

1. **Right-to-Win statement on slide 02** — the three specific reasons this firm wins this engagement: typically (a) a category capability the client has explicitly named, (b) a partner-level practitioner with prior context, (c) a methodology or asset that compresses time-to-value. Generic claims ("global presence", "industry experience") are rewritten or removed.
2. **Proof Stack on slides 06-08** — three named comparable engagements (client name OR sanitized to "a Global 50 chemicals manufacturer"), each with: situation, our work, outcome (quantified), the partner who led it, and a reference contact (offered). One Proof slide per case. The deck never makes a capability claim without naming a comparable.
3. **Team Slide with Real Partners on slide 13** — the actual partners + senior advisors who will work the engagement, with their relevant prior engagement context. Bench-strength logos are not allowed unless they are tied to a named individual on the team.
